Paper · 2026

Breath, Land, and Sovereignty

Abstract

Turning Stone Resort Casino, established by the Oneida Indian Nation in 1993, is frequently framed within a narrative of commercial gaming and economic development. This paper argues that Turning Stone is an enduring vernacular, Indigenous-authored landmark. Through an Oneida worldview, the resort is a site of cultural resurgence rather than accommodation, where space carries substantial responsibility. The land remembers and it tells a story.

The paper is grounded in Indigenous methodologies prioritizing relational accountability. The Indigenous canon rejects the western narrative of landscape and architectural theories set forth by J.B. Jackson, Upton, Van Slyck, and Swentzell. The architecture at Turning Stone immersively teaches: the Lodge draws on the longhouse as a model of kinship and governance; the Skʌ:nʌ́ (Spa) functions as a sanctuary for spiritual health. The public use of Oneida language throughout the resort implies an Indigenous presence that is held honorably.

The research continues to discuss these inseparable relationship practices from the legal geography of federal trust land, where repossession of sovereignty becomes visible. Turning Stone's influence in culture, language revitalization, and agriculture links contemporary enterprise to ancestral immersion.

This paper is written from the position of an Indigenous scholar and a descendant of Shenandoah, which honors the heritage of the land and treats positionality as responsibility. Turning Stone exists to assert presence for descendants. The excellence of Turning Stone is not an act, but a habitat insisting that Indigenous sovereignty is ongoing — one that breathes life into its soil across generations.
